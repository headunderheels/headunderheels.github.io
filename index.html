Context

Spatial Audio is an emerging technology that allows for an immersive listening experience in headphones and earbuds. Companies that develop consumer audio products, like Apple and Bose, have now worked this technology into their product ecosystems, incorporating on-board gyroscopes and accelerometers to track the motion of the head in space, allowing for some neat audio trickery.

How it Works

Stereo hearing can be likened to stereo vision — just as having two eyes allows us to percieve visual depth, having two ears allows us to perceive sonic orientation.

There are various cues which allow the ear and the brain to estimate the origin of a sound in space. When you hear a sound — like an angry stranger yelling at you from a car — your brain can place this person in your surroundings by looking at several simple psychoacoustic cues.

1. Volume Attenuation

When a sound approaches the head from the side, much of the sound is absorbed by the mass of the head, resulting in a loudness reduction in the other ear.

This volume attenuation can be achieved using a panning potentiometer, and is not enough to sell the immersion of spatial audio.

A pan-pot is a device used to place a sound left-to-right on a soundstage

2. Time Delay

Since sound does not travel instantaneously, when you hear a sound in your environment that originates off-center, the sound will reach each one ear first, then the other a short time later. This difference in timing is one of the most crucial auditory cues indicating direction, and is known as the interaural time difference (ITD).

A person with a head width of 20cm will experience an ITD of roughly 5 milliseconds for sounds originating from the side of the head.

3. Attenuation and Resonance

As a sound passes through the head or ear tissues, higher frequencies are generally selectively blocked, filtered, attenuated, by those tissues. Thus, a sound approaching one ear will generally sound "duller" in the other ear, as it has been filtered by the head. The same is true for both ears when a sound approaches from the rear.

The ear canal and head also have various resonating structures, which may impact the frequency makeup of the sound in one ear versus the other. However, modeling just the filtering effects does a very good job at selling the illusion of direction present in spatial audio.

4. Reverberation

Reverberation is a property of nearly every space where sound exists — even outdoors. As a sound spreads around a space, it repeatedly reflects off of surfaces it hits, then scattering.

Anechoic chambers are spaces designed to minimize sound reflection reverberation by optimizing the absorption of sound.
Thunder is caused by the reverberation of an auditory impulse from lightning; this is partly why thunder rumbles at a distance, and sounds transient when it strikes your house and blows it up.

Particularly indoors, reverberation cues us in to sound direction because it manifests the shape of our surroundings in sound. Sounds which approach one ear will reverberate off of opposing walls before approaching the other ear, incurring a further delay time.

A Note on Generality and Efficiency

The specific acoustic properties described here will vary from person to person, and from ear to ear. Everyone has a differently shaped head, so it is often futile and computationally expensive to attempt to fully model the resonant and attenuative intricacies of the ear canal. In practice, Spatial Audio models the head using a Head-Related Transfer Function (HRTF), which approximates the aforemented acoustic properties of the head. In general, this does a very good job at selling the effect, as you will hear in the following section.
